# beyond the marquee: the second tier worth your time

the landscape essay was written off the first 100-org tier — genentech, the big pharma data-science programs, the brand-name techbio, the elite institutes. that read still holds; it is the strategy. but the vault has since grown more than six-fold, to 620 organizations, across successive broader tiers. this post is the map of those tiers: four categories the first pass under-counted, what they are actually worth to an HRD / single-cell / spatial cancer-omics PhD on an F-1, and where the rare F-1-actionable seats hide.

the honesty caveat from the landscape post still applies to every row here: as of mid-2026 the summer-2027 cycle mostly is not posted yet, so these are 2026-cycle roles and recurring programs that show you the shape and the eligibility, not live 2027 links.

## mid-size and specialty oncology / genomics — the sleeper value

this is the category most worth a second look. below the top-20 pharma names sits a deep bench of mid-size and specialty shops that run real, well-scoped comp-bio internships and draw a smaller applicant pool than genentech. **biomarin's computational-biology PhD intern** is the clearest example — a 12-week role building algorithms for context-specific drug mechanism-of-action and bridging foundation models across biological modalities, which reads almost exactly like the marquee genentech tracks but with far less name-recognition competition. **ionis** runs an annual NGS-and-functional-genomics summer intern (wet-dry, transcriptomics + long-read sequencing) and clinical-data-science and AI tracks alongside it. the cell-therapy and gene-editing cohort — beam, intellia, crispr therapeutics, caribou, prime, arsenalbio — increasingly has informatics / AI-foundry teams that host interns.

the F-1 read here is the same as marquee pharma: private-sector funding, so eligibility is usually a `verify` rather than a wall, and you confirm CPT/OPT acceptance per req. apply on genuine fit, but apply — the conversion rate for your profile is good and the field is thinner.

## diagnostics, sequencing and lab-tools — the pipeline play

the first tier badly under-counted this sector; it is now 30 orgs. natera, exact sciences, myriad, neogenomics on the clinical-genomics side; thermo fisher, agilent, pacbio, twist, bio-rad on the platform side; and the spatial / single-cell tool makers — vizgen, parse, scale, akoya — whose science is literally the assays you analyze. these run real bioinformatics and computational-genomics intern pipelines, and your NGS / single-cell skill set maps onto them cleanly. two honest caveats: the work skews toward production pipelines and clinical-genomics rather than open-ended discovery modeling, and visa stance is almost always unstated (`verify`). treat this as a breadth play — solid, fundable, skill-relevant — rather than a bullseye.

## cancer centers and health systems — read the fine print

this is the category where the eligibility flag matters most, because two very different things live under one roof. **institution-funded PhD lab internships** at mayo, cleveland clinic, city of hope, moffitt, mount sinai, penn, hopkins, and the university cancer centers are usually fine for an F-1 — you join a computational lab for the summer on your own work authorization. **federally-funded summer internship programs** at the same institutions are frequently NIH/NSF citizen-or-PR-only, and this vault flags those `✗`. the single best illustration of getting it right sits in the marquee tier already: **MSK's computer science internship program** states eligibility as "US citizen, permanent resident, or visa holder allowing work in the US" — that last clause explicitly includes an F-1 on valid CPT/OPT, which is why it earns a `✓`. the lesson generalizes: at a cancer center, the program's own eligibility sentence is the whole ballgame. read it before you invest an application.

## nonprofit institutes and newer techbio — lottery tickets, played on fit

arc institute, altos, salk, whitehead, gladstone, the parker institute, plus the newer AI-bio startups (ginkgo, cradle, chroma, tessera, newlimit) — scientifically these are some of the most aligned places in the entire vault, and structurally they are the hardest to convert cold. tiny teams, one or two seats, often no formal program, and entry usually through a warm intro or a paper the group already knows. the move is the same as the marquee techbio advice: pick a small number on real fit, open with a method or a paper rather than a generic resume, and accept that the base rate is low.

## the F-1 gem worth internalizing

here is the most useful single finding from this tier: some second-tier biotechs are *more* F-1-actionable than the marquee names, because they say so out loud. **caribou biosciences'** summer bioinformatics intern posting explicitly states that foreign students may apply with OPT approval from their school. that is a rare, earned `✓` — and it would have been invisible if the search had stopped at the top 100. the takeaway is not "caribou specifically"; it is that the confirmed-eligible seats are scattered across the second tier, and the only way to find them is to read the actual postings rather than assume the big names are the only real options.

## how to use this tier

do not carpet-bomb it. the second tier is where you spend judgment, not volume:

1. **work the mid-size onco/genomics names like a smaller version of the pharma spine** — biomarin, ionis, and the specialty shops where your background converts and the pool is thinner.
2. **add a few diagnostics/tools names for breadth**, knowing the work is pipeline-leaning and the visa stance needs verifying.
3. **at cancer centers, filter to institution-funded lab internships and read every eligibility sentence** — skip the federal SIPs.
4. **treat institutes and new techbio as fit-driven lottery tickets**, a handful each, opened with a method.
5. **prioritize the rare confirmed-eligible seats** (caribou-style `✓` rows) wherever they appear — those are worth more of your attention than a marquee name that will never clarify your visa status.

filter resources by sector and by the topics you actually do; the jobs vault covers full-time roles and postdocs (US and international). the marquee tier is still the spine — this tier is how you widen the funnel without lowering the bar.
