# the F-1 playbook: CPT, OPT, and the mistake that costs you a year

this is not legal advice, and the only authority that can actually authorize your work is your school's designated school official (DSO). but the mechanics below are stable and worth understanding cold, because one of the choices here is irreversible and most students make it without realizing there was a choice.

## the two doors: CPT and pre-completion OPT

an F-1 student can work a paid US internship through one of two mechanisms.

**curricular practical training (CPT)** ties the work to your curriculum. it must relate directly to your major, be an integral part of your established program, and — critically — you must secure the specific position first and then get DSO authorization on an updated I-20 *before* you start. CPT cannot be backdated. you generally need to have completed at least one full academic year of full-time enrollment before a DSO can authorize CPT, though graduate programs whose curriculum requires earlier training can be an exception.

**pre-completion optional practical training (OPT)** is the other door. it caps you at 20 hours/week while school is in session but permits full-time work during breaks — including summer. that makes it a natural fit for a summer internship.

## the trap that makes the choice matter

here is the part almost nobody flags until it is too late: **using 365 days or more of full-time CPT (more than 20 hours/week) at a given degree level permanently eliminates your eligibility for post-completion OPT — and the STEM extension — at that level.** that is the 12-to-36 months of work authorization you were counting on after you graduate. part-time CPT (20 hours/week or less) does not count toward that 364-day total and does not affect OPT.

a single full-time summer on CPT is only ~12 weeks, well under the 365-day cliff, so one summer will not by itself burn your OPT. the danger is cumulative: multiple full-time CPT summers, or a year-long full-time CPT, can push you over, and the loss is permanent and silent.

## so what should you actually do

for a one-summer internship, **pre-completion OPT is usually the safer default**: it gives you full-time summer authorization without spending any of your CPT-to-OPT budget, so your post-graduation OPT and STEM extension stay fully intact. the tradeoff is lead time — pre-completion OPT is adjudicated by USCIS and takes months, so you apply well before the summer, not after you sign the offer.

CPT is faster (your DSO authorizes it, no USCIS wait) and is the right tool when the timeline is tight or the role is tightly tied to your curriculum — just keep it part-time, or keep your cumulative full-time CPT comfortably under a year, and you preserve OPT.

the practical sequence either way: talk to your DSO *now*, before you even apply, so you know which door your school supports and how long it takes; secure the offer; get the authorization in hand before day one.

## who you are eligible for — and who you are not

most private-sector internships (the pharma, techbio, and big-tech roles that make up the bulk of this vault) require only that you be legally authorized to work, which CPT or OPT satisfies. genentech's own FAQ is representative: all internships are paid so you must be authorized to work, they accept international students, and sponsorship support varies by position. that is a green light for an F-1 on CPT/OPT.

two categories are different, and this vault flags them:

- **explicit no-sponsorship programs.** a handful of corporate programs state that candidates must be permanently authorized and that CPT/OPT does not qualify — novartis's analytics PhD program is the clearest example. these are marked with a ✗ and are not worth an application.
- **federal and DOE national labs.** oak ridge, berkeley/JGI, argonne, PNNL, los alamos, and the NIH federal summer internship program generally require US citizenship or permanent residency. these are marked ✗ as well. the one nuance: NIH's graduate partnerships program sometimes accepts international students enrolled at US institutions — verify directly, do not assume.

## the badges in this vault

every role carries an F-1 flag: **✓** means a posting or FAQ explicitly accepts international/CPT/OPT students; **?** means the posting is silent and you should verify per posting (this is most roles, and silence is not a no); **✗** means citizenship/PR is required or the program explicitly excludes sponsorship-dependent students. the flagging is deliberately conservative — a ✓ is earned, not assumed.
