> *The fourth turn around the [fifty-concepts](how-vc-foundation-models-grew-up.md) device — the same fifty numbered concepts, each in bold and carried once, but reorganized under a new spine. v1 was a neutral map; [v2 became a bet](fifty-concepts-one-bet-v2.md); the [four-acts companion](how-vc-foundation-models-grew-up.md) read the field as a history. This version reads it as **five questions we ask a cell**, each a rung higher than the last. The deep-dives still live elsewhere: the [reckoning](why-linear-baselines-win.md), the [diffusion/flow explainer](diffusion-and-flow-matching-for-virtual-cells.md), the [causal-models survey](causal-models-fm-and-vc.md), and the [survival-field model](what-disappears-conditional-viability-v3.md) this ladder climbs toward.*

Every computational biologist is chasing the same dream, even when we don't say it out loud: to rebuild a living cell inside a computer — not a cartoon of one, but a cell you can *query*. Pin down what "query" means and the dream resolves into five questions, each harder than the last. What does a typical cell look like? Which genes move together, and in what programs? What happens when you hit it with a signal — a drug, a cytokine? Where will it be in ten days? And, at the top of the ladder: I know where it's headed — what do I do to send it somewhere else? The history of virtual-cell models is the climb up these five rungs, and the recurring mistake is taking one rung for another. Fifty concepts, in order, carried once each.

## Rung 1 — DESCRIBE: give a cell coordinates

A **(1) foundation model (FM)** is a high-capacity network pretrained once on a large unlabeled corpus and then adapted to many tasks; the **(2) virtual cell (VC)** is the prize at the end — a computational cell you can ask "what if." The raw material is the **(3) single-cell atlas** (CELLxGENE, Tabula Sapiens) — an observational catalog of which states cells happen to occupy. The recipe was borrowed wholesale from language models: **(4) self-supervised pretraining** on millions of transcriptomes, **(5) tokenization** turning a cell's expression counts into a finite vocabulary, and **(6) masked-gene modeling** — hide some of a cell's *genes*, predict them, and learn a representation as a side effect (note: it masks genes, not cell-type labels). The wave followed quickly: **(7) scBERT**, **(8) Geneformer**, **(9) scGPT**, **(10) scFoundation**, **(11) UCE**, **(12) CellPLM** — TranscriptFormer later carrying the same representation ambition across species. What each really delivered was a **(13) frozen encoder**: pretrain once, then read out coordinates for any new cell with no further training. By the end of rung one, a cell had coordinates.

## Rung 2 — CORRELATE: read the programs

The next question is which genes move together. The embeddings encode **(14) gene programs** — co-expression modules the network learned without being told to — and on this rung the FMs proved genuinely good at **(15) embed-and-classify**: sorting cells by type and state, integrating batches, annotating. To read the programs back out of the black box, **(16) sparse autoencoders (SAEs)** decompose the latent into **(17) monosemantic features** — interpretable directions, one biological concept apiece. The verdict that emerged from prying the box open is the same one the next rung will deliver the hard way: the features encode organized biological *knowledge*, but minimal causal *mechanism*. Rungs one and two are where foundation models shine — and where the trouble began, because the field mistook coordinates for understanding.

## The hinge — RECKON: coordinates are not predictions

The test that mattered was the **(18) perturbation-prediction task**: given a drug or a gene knockout, predict the cell's new state. In 2025 a **(19) linear baseline** — predict the mean of the training perturbations, no FM at all — was shown by the **(20) reckoning** of Ahlmann-Eltze & Huber to match or beat every published single-cell FM on exactly this task. Part of the gap was methodological — **(21) leaky splits** and metrics that rewarded predicting the population mean — and part was real. The **(22) 2026 re-appraisal** kept FMs for what they are good at (rungs one and two, once fine-tuned) and stopped pretending the masked-gene objective had learned regulatory logic. The diagnosis was clean: representation is not intervention, and you cannot climb rung three for free.

## Rung 3 — INTERVENE: what a signal does

So the field changed its data, turning from **(23) observational to interventional data** — and intervention comes in two flavors that are easy to conflate: **genetic** perturbation via **(24) Perturb-seq** (single-cell readout paired with CRISPR edits) and **chemical** perturbation via drug atlases like **(25) Tahoe-100M** (~100M cells, ~1,100 compounds, 50 cancer lines, dose-resolved). On this new substrate came perturbation-native models — Arc Institute's **(26) STATE** and the 3-billion-parameter **(27) Tahoe-x1** — STATE's signature move being to model the **(28) set-level distributional shift** a perturbation induces rather than a single point prediction. The benchmarks softened, too: under **(29) well-calibrated metrics**, the newer and larger models begin to beat the linear baselines on rung three, though by *how much* is still argued. Two lessons — that scale is not the only axis, and that a smaller, well-fine-tuned model with the right context can beat a giant — split the field into a **(30) two-direction era**: large institutes scaling foundation models, smaller groups of statisticians and computational scientists innovating on the modeling itself, with **(31) Pertpy** standardizing the analysis pipeline underneath both.

## Rung 4 — PREDICT: draw the cell forward

If a point prediction loses to a ridge regression, raise the ambition: model the whole **(32) distribution** of outcomes and sample from it. The machinery came straight from computer vision — **(33) diffusion**, **(34) flow matching**, the **(35) diffusion transformer (DiT)** — transplanted into biology. Generating raw 20,000-dimensional counts is brutal, so **(36) latent diffusion** runs the process inside a learned latent and a **(37) count-aware decoder** (a negative-binomial head) restores the discrete, over-dispersed structure of real scRNA-seq; **(38) classifier-free guidance** steers samples toward a drug or dose. The single-cell instances followed: **(39) CFGen**, **(40) scDiffusion**, and **(41) CellFlow** (with scDiffEq making the diffusion *be* the cell's dynamics, and adjacent tools like RegVelo feeding the same goal from the velocity side). And here the rung earns its caveat: **(42) distribution-matching is not causality** — a generator can reproduce the treated population beautifully and still not know why any single cell moved.

## Rung 5 — COUNTERFACTUAL: change its fate

The honest response to that caveat is to model the *effect* directly — Pearl's **(43) do() operator**: not P(cell) but P(cell | *do*(drug)). Two moves are emerging. One disentangles the effect: **(44) CPA** factors a cell's latent into additive perturbation, dose, and covariate components (chemCPA routing the perturbation through a molecular-structure encoder so it extrapolates to unseen drugs); **(45) GEARS** injects a gene–gene knowledge graph to predict unseen and combinatorial knockouts. The other transports the population: the **(46) optimal-transport coupling** — Waddington-OT, CellOT, moscot — reads a perturbation as a map carrying control cells to treated ones, a **(47) per-cell treatment effect**. Push that to its sharpest form and the question flips: not *what does a survivor look like* but **who survives** — the **(48) survival field** π(*x* | drug, dose), the probability a cell at state *x* persists under that treatment. Modeling survival rather than appearance forces a distinction the generators quietly skip — **(49) competing risks**, telling true death apart from a non-lethal change of state. And the axis the first four rungs mostly ignore is **(50) dynamics**: today's models are snapshots; the next is time.

## So where are we, and which rung is next

We have powerful models (STATE), giant interventional atlases (Tahoe-100M), and standardized pipelines (Pertpy). On the five-rung ladder, the field has climbed solidly through rung four and is just setting hands on rung five. The mistake would be to think the next breakthrough is a bigger foundation model. It isn't. The next rung is a change in the *object* we model — from describing the cell to deciding its fate, from existence to survival. Closest in spirit are the optimal-transport models with growth-and-death terms and the selection coefficients of CRISPR screens, but no one has yet assembled a learned, drug- and dose-conditioned, per-cell survival field that separates death from transition and generalizes to unseen drugs; CPA's own authors admit their models "cannot capture cell viability or abundance." That gap is the opportunity. Build the model that knows who lives, and you finally have a virtual cell worth querying: not one that tells you what a treated population looks like, but one that tells you what to *do*.
