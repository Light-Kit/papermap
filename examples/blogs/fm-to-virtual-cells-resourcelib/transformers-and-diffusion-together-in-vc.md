> *A follow-on to [diffusion and flow matching for virtual cells](diffusion-and-flow-matching-for-virtual-cells.md), which explains what those generative families are. This one answers a question that keeps getting framed as a fight — "did diffusion beat the transformer?" — by showing they are layers of one stack, not contestants. For the surrounding vocabulary see the [fifty-concepts bet](fifty-concepts-one-bet-v2.md); for what people actually reuse, [what researchers build on](what-researchers-actually-build-on.md); for where this lands as a model, the [survival-field essay](what-disappears-conditional-viability-v3.md).*

## The duel is a category error

Ask "did diffusion win over transformers in virtual cells" and you have already mixed two different things. A **transformer** is an *architecture* — a function approximator built from attention, the thing that turns a set of gene tokens into a vector. **Diffusion** (and its cousin flow matching) is a *training objective and sampling process* — a way to learn a probability distribution and draw from it. One is the engine block; the other is what the engine is tuned to do. You can build a diffusion model whose network is a transformer, a CNN, or an MLP, and you can train a transformer with a masked-token loss, a contrastive loss, or a diffusion loss. So "transformer vs diffusion" is like asking whether the engine beat the fuel. The interesting question is how they fit together — and in virtual cells, they fit at four distinct layers.

## Layer 1 — the denoiser is usually a transformer

The most direct way they combine: the network *inside* the diffusion model is a transformer. The Diffusion Transformer ([Peebles & Xie, ICCV 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.09748)) showed you can throw out the convolutional U-Net that early diffusion models used and replace it with a plain transformer that attends over the (noised) input plus the timestep and conditioning — and that this scales better. In flow matching the same holds: the **velocity field** is parameterized by a transformer. For cells this is natural, because a cell is already a set of gene tokens, exactly the input attention was built for. So when you read that a cell generator is "a diffusion model," look one level down and you almost always find a transformer doing the denoising. They are not alternatives at this layer; one is wrapped inside the other.

## Layer 2 — diffusion runs in the transformer's latent space

The second combination is latent diffusion. Generating raw ~20,000-dimensional sparse count vectors directly is hard, so the trick borrowed from image generation ([Rombach et al., CVPR 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.10752)) is to generate in a *learned latent space* and decode back out. In cells, that latent space is exactly what a pretrained transformer foundation model gives you: run scGPT, Geneformer, UCE, or Tahoe-x1 as a frozen encoder, do the diffusion or flow matching in their embedding space, and decode with a count-aware head so the output respects the discrete, over-dispersed structure of real scRNA-seq. **CFGen** ([Palma et al., ICLR 2025](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.11734)) and **scDiffusion** ([Luo et al., *Bioinformatics* 2024](https://academic.oup.com/bioinformatics/article/40/9/btae518/7738782)) both lean on learned latents and count-aware likelihoods rather than treating expression as Gaussian. Here the division is crisp: the transformer supplies the *coordinate system* — a compact manifold where distances mean something — and diffusion supplies the *generative motion* on it.

## Layer 3 — transformers do the conditioning

A virtual cell is only useful if you can steer it: generate a cell *given* a drug, a perturbation, a cell type, or a niche. That steering vector almost always comes from a transformer. A small-molecule drug is encoded by a chemistry transformer like **MoLFormer** ([Ross et al., 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.09553)); a cell's context is encoded by a cell-FM transformer; these vectors are then injected into the generator and used for **classifier-free guidance** ([Ho & Salimans, 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.12598)) — train with and without the label, then at sampling time push generation toward the condition by interpolating the two. The generative model provides the mechanism for *moving* probability mass; the transformer encoders provide the *direction* to move it. Neither does the other's job.

## Layer 4 — the real division of labor: represent, then transport

Step back and the two answer complementary questions. The transformer is built to answer **where can a cell be, and what is this cell** — attention over genes yields identity, cell type, batch, a usable representation; this is the embed-and-classify strength the first foundation-model wave was built on ([scGPT](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-024-02201-0), [Geneformer](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06139-9)). Diffusion and flow matching are built to answer **how does a cell move, and what would it become** — transport along the manifold, conditional sampling of a counterfactual population ([CellFlow](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.04.11.648220v1) for population responses; [scDiffEq](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2023.12.06.570508) making the diffusion *be* the dynamics). The working pipeline is therefore **embed-then-generate**: a transformer maps cells into a latent, a generative model transports within it, and a decoder returns counts. Arc's **STATE** ([2025](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.06.26.661135)) is a shipped instance of exactly this split — a state-representation module and a state-transition module — rather than a bet on one family over the other.

## Each covers the other's blind spot

This is why the framing should be cooperation, not competition. A transformer trained to embed-and-classify has **no usable density** — it learns a decision boundary, not a distribution, so it cannot tell you how likely a proposed cell is or sample a new one; the masked-token objective never produced a generative model. Diffusion gives it exactly that. Conversely, a diffusion model dropped onto raw counts with no good coordinate system and no conditioning is slow, blurry, and unsteerable; the transformer gives it a manifold to work in and a direction to be pushed. Put crudely: the transformer knows *what cells are*, diffusion knows *how to make more and move between them*, and the virtual-cell stack needs both. The predecessor that tried to do both inside one model — the VAE, [scVI](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-018-0229-2) — is precisely what the modern split improved on: same generative goal, but blurrier samples and a looser density than a dedicated diffusion/flow head delivers.

## The caveat the reckoning leaves standing

Composing them does not buy causality. A transformer-encoded latent with a flow-matching generator and transformer conditioning can match the distribution of treated cells beautifully and still not predict the response to a held-out drug, because **density is not causality** and the joint distribution is not the regulatory mechanism — the same wall the [linear-baseline reckoning](why-linear-baselines-win.md) ran into. The combined stack is necessary, not sufficient: it is the best machinery the field has for representing and transporting cell state, but a beautifully cooperating transformer-plus-diffusion model still has to clear a tuned linear baseline before any of its elegance counts. Architecture cooperation is not a substitute for the right inductive bias.

## Where this lands for us

Our own bet is a deliberate instance of this stack, not a side in the duel. The [survival-field model](what-disappears-conditional-viability-v3.md) freezes a transformer cell encoder (Tahoe-x1) and a transformer drug encoder (MoLFormer) for the representation layer, runs a flow / transport generator for the movement layer, and adds the one thing neither family supplies — a death term that decides *who survives* rather than only what survivors look like. That is the answer to "how do these two work together": the transformer gives the coordinates and the conditioning, the diffusion/flow gives the transport, and the science you add on top — the inductive bias the reckoning says is missing — is what turns a cooperating pair of general-purpose tools into a virtual cell. Stop asking which won; ask what you bolt onto the pair.
