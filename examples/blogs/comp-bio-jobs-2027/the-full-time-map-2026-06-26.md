# the full-time map: where a PRC-national comp-bio PhD can actually land a job

This is the full-time companion to the US-internships vault. It tracks **947 employers and 1,187 roles** — industry full-time jobs (FTE) and academic postdocs — across the US and four international regions, read for a computational-biology PhD with a Chinese (PRC) passport, currently in a US program, who will be looking for a real job in the **2027–2029** window. The filter here is **United States vs International**, because the thing that reorders the whole map for full-time work is not the science — it is the visa.

The one sentence that changes everything versus the internship vault: **for full-time work the screen is visa sponsorship, not CPT/OPT.** An internship runs on your student status. A job runs on whether an employer will carry you through OPT into something durable — and that question has a different answer in San Francisco, in Basel, and in Shanghai.

## the US visa math you are actually solving

After you defend, you get **OPT (12 months) + STEM OPT (24 months) = a 36-month runway** to work in the US on your student visa. That window is generous, but it ends. Before it does, an employer has to sponsor an **H-1B** (a lottery — not guaranteed even with a willing employer) or start a green card. So in the US vault every role carries a **Visa-sponsor** flag, and the map splits into three honest groups:

- **The cap-exempt path (the strongest one, and the one people underrate).** US **universities and nonprofit research institutes are H-1B cap-exempt** — they sponsor H-1B *outside* the lottery, plus J-1, year-round. That makes an **academic postdoc the most reliable full-time foothold** in the US for a foreign national, not a consolation prize. The vault flags **199 US postdocs `✓`** on this basis — at MSK, Dana-Farber, the Broad, Fred Hutch, Penn, the cancer centers — and many are dead-on for a single-cell / spatial / HRD background. If you want certainty of being able to *stay and work*, this is it.
- **The industry sponsors (most of big pharma and big tech).** Large, established employers sponsor H-1B routinely; **321 US roles are flagged `✓` in total — 199 cap-exempt postdocs plus 122 sponsoring-industry roles**, and the on-profile ones are real: Genentech gRED Computational Sciences, Novartis Oncology Data Science, AbbVie Oncology Bioinformatics, BMS Computational Genomics, Merck, Pfizer, Amgen, the NVIDIA/Illumina/Tempus tier. The title to target is the *first* PhD rung — "Scientist," "Bioinformatics Scientist," "Computational Biologist I" — even when the post says "Senior," because PhD+2 often clears their bar.
- **Verify, and the hard no's.** **251 US roles sit at `?`** — mostly startups that simply do not state a sponsorship policy; ask in the first screen, because a small company that won't file an H-1B is a dead end no matter how good the science. And **9 are `✗`**: DOE national labs and federal roles (citizenship/clearance), and **T32-funded training postdocs** (Van Andel, UCLA, Case) which by statute require US citizens or permanent residents. Those are flagged so you don't spend the application.

## industry FTE vs postdoc — the real trade

For you specifically the two paths are not equal on immigration. An **industry FTE** pays far more and is closer to your internship instincts, but its US immigration runs through the H-1B lottery — a coin-flip you don't control. A **postdoc** pays academic wages, but it is cap-exempt, so the work-authorization question is essentially solved, and it keeps the door open to the O-1 / EB-1 "extraordinary ability" track that strong publication records make reachable. The pragmatic move is to **run both pipelines at once**: chase the sponsoring-industry Scientist roles, but keep two or three cap-exempt cancer-genomics postdocs as the floor you can always stand on.

## international — the same question, different answers

Outside the US, F-1/OPT is irrelevant; the flag becomes host-country work authorization for a PRC national. The 606 international roles split sharply:

- **China (125 roles, essentially all `✓`).** No work-visa barrier at all, and the comp-bio depth is real: BeiGene/BeOne computational science, ByteDance and Tencent AI-for-science, WuXi, BGI/MGI, Zai Lab, Insilico, plus the clinical-genomics shops. This is the one region where your work authorization is simply not a question.
- **UK & Europe (254 roles).** The most prestigious science and the hardest fine print for a non-EU national. The international-by-design hosts are the bullseyes — **EMBL/EMBL-EBI, DKFZ, the Crick, Roche/Novartis Basel** (which relocate international hires) — while **AstraZeneca-UK and GSK** now read `?` (both are licensed UK Skilled Worker sponsors, so ask — it is posting-dependent). Most of the rest is `?`: the science is excellent, the gate is whether the institute will carry the permit.
- **Singapore / Japan / Korea (117) and Canada / Australia (110).** A*STAR and RIKEN are international-friendly and on-profile for genomics; Canada's research-worker permits are comparatively navigable (AbCellera, the Toronto AI-bio cluster, OICR, BC Cancer). Australia's flagship institutes (Garvan, WEHI, Peter Mac) do superb cancer work but read `?` on visa support for an external hire.

## what I think you should do

1. **Make the cap-exempt postdoc your floor.** It is the single most reliable way for you to hold a full-time US position, and the cancer-genomics fit is genuinely strong. Line up two or three (MSK, Fred Hutch, Broad, a DFCI lab) early.
2. **Run the sponsoring-industry FTE pipeline in parallel** — Genentech, Novartis, AbbVie, BMS, Merck — targeting the first PhD rung, and treat every `?` startup as "ask about sponsorship in call one."
3. **Keep China as the no-friction option** if you are open to going back — the depth is real and the visa question vanishes.
4. **In Europe, go straight for the international-by-design institutes** (EMBL, DKFZ, Crick, Basel) and skip the stated-no-sponsorship walls.
5. **Settle the visa question before the offer, every time** — the same discipline the internship playbook urged for CPT/OPT now applies to H-1B and cap-exemption.

Filter by United States vs International and by the topics you actually do, and start with the starred roles. The US-internships vault is the separate, near-term track; this one is for the job on the other side of the defense.
