# The US biopharma landscape

There are two honest ways to read this slice of the vault, and they point in opposite
directions.

Read it as a scientist and it is encouraging: **78% of these roles are cancer-omics
roles.** Your exact biology, in demand, right now. Read it as someone who needs an H-1B
and it is sobering: the part of the market that most wants your science is the part
least likely to have ever sponsored anyone.

That tension is the whole post.

## What "the market" actually is

Filtering the vault to the US, to `kind = position`, and to biopharma proper — pharma and
big biotech, plus ML-first TechBio — leaves **255 roles across 206 employers.**

| Sector | Roles | Confirmed sponsor (✓) | Unverified (?) | % ✓ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| ML-first biotech (TechBio) | 181 | 34 | 147 | **19%** |
| Pharma / big biotech | 74 | 47 | 27 | **64%** |
| **US biopharma total** | **255** | **81** | **174** | **32%** |

For context, the rest of the US vault: academic/institute is 205 roles at **97% ✓**
(university and nonprofit H-1Bs are cap-exempt — no lottery); diagnostics/tools is 89 at
24%; big tech bio/health is 22 at 95%; national labs are 10 roles with **zero** confirmed
sponsors and the only explicit refusals anywhere in the data.

## The finding

TechBio has **2.4× the openings** of big pharma — 181 to 74. It is where the
foundation-model work is, where the compute is, and where the science reads like your
feed. And it converts to just **34 confirmed-sponsor roles**, against pharma's **47**.

The smaller sector contains *more* sponsoring roles in absolute terms. TechBio's size
advantage doesn't shrink under the visa filter — it inverts.

This is not hostility. A 40-person Series A company that has never filed a labor
condition application simply has no immigration function, and you would be asking it to
build one for its first computational hire.

## Read the checkmark correctly

This is the most important paragraph here, so it gets its own section.

A **✓ means the employer has a track record of sponsoring.** It does *not* mean the
posting promises sponsorship. The vault says so in its own notes — Bayer's spatial role,
the single best-matched posting in the file, carries the note *"sponsorship not stated but
Bayer is an established H-1B sponsor."*

And a **? means unverified, not refused.** Across all 255 biopharma roles there are
**zero** explicit refusals. The 174 question marks are not closed doors. They are doors
nobody has knocked on.

## The funnel

The 255 are not 255 opportunities. Four filters, each non-negotiable for you:

| Filter | Roles left |
|---|---:|
| All US biopharma | 255 |
| …and cancer-omics core (single-cell ∪ spatial ∪ comp-onc ∪ genomics ∪ multi-omics) | 199 |
| …and a confirmed sponsor | **70** |
| …and live right now | 34 |
| …and on your topics (single-cell / spatial / comp-onc) | **22** — across 19 employers |

Sit with row three. Your science is wanted in 199 roles. Your paperwork is wanted in 70.
**The visa filter is the most destructive cut in your entire search** — more destructive
than seniority, geography, or subfield. Any strategy that doesn't put it first is
optimizing the wrong variable.

## Your bottleneck is information, not résumé

174 of 255 roles are question marks. Every one you personally resolve to ✓ hands you a
target nobody else competing for it has bothered to qualify. Resolve them cheapest-first:

1. **Public filing records — free, anonymous, decisive.** H-1B petitions and labor
   condition applications are public record. The USCIS H-1B Employer Data Hub and the DOL
   LCA disclosure files will tell you whether a company has filed for scientists in the
   last three years. One that has is functionally a ✓. One that never has is functionally
   a ✗ *no matter what a recruiter tells you.* Do this before you write a single cover
   letter.
2. **Ask an employee** — ideally another PRC-national scientist on the team. One question:
   *did the company file for you?*
3. **Ask the recruiter**, never the hiring manager, and only after the first positive
   signal. Lead with what you *don't* need: *"I have roughly three years of OPT and
   STEM-OPT work authorization, so I can start without sponsorship. For planning — does the
   company file H-1B petitions for scientists when that time comes?"* You are asking about
   year four while telling them you're free in year one.
4. **Answer the ATS sponsorship checkbox truthfully. Always.** It is the one mistake that
   cannot be undone.

## What the market is buying

| Topic | Roles | Share |
|---|---:|---:|
| ml-ai | 186 | 73% |
| genomics | 164 | 64% |
| drug-discovery | 109 | 43% |
| comp-onc | 101 | 40% |
| single-cell | 74 | 29% |
| foundation-models | 51 | 20% |
| clinical-genomics | 33 | 13% |
| **spatial** | **9** | **4%** |

**ml-ai at 73% is not a differentiator — it is a filter.** Saying "I use machine learning"
in 2027 says nothing about you.

And spatial — the specialty — appears in **nine roles**. Four carry a ✓; three of the five
question marks aren't even live requisitions. A job search built on the sentence *"I am a
spatial transcriptomics person"* is a search against nine doors.

But spatial isn't a liability. It is being *mis-sold*. Look at what those nine roles
co-tag: single-cell, comp-onc, genomics, pathology-imaging. Nobody in this market buys
"spatial." They buy **multimodal integration of high-dimensional, batch-confounded tissue
data** — which is what spatial work actually proves you can do. So lead with cancer
multi-omics and ML (a 199-role market) and deploy spatial as *depth*: evidence you can
handle a hard, noisy, new modality. That is a hiring signal, not a keyword.

**The translation that pays best:** clinical-genomics is only 33 roles — but **16 of them
are confirmed sponsors. That 48% is the best sponsorship rate of any topic slice in the
data**, well above the 32% baseline. Pharma does not buy "HRD." It buys *who gets the PARP
inhibitor.* Translational bioinformatics is the one place where the thesis topic and the
visa odds point the same direction.

**And a warning on foundation models.** They appear in 51 roles — but **49 of the 51 are
TechBio**, with only 10 confirmed sponsors, and they skew toward proteins and molecules
rather than cells and tumors. Chase the FM label and you walk into the corner of the market
with the worst sponsorship rate *and* compete against people who've done generative
chemistry for five years. Meanwhile **69 roles carry no ml-ai tag at all** — a rigorous
translational bioinformatician who isn't an ML researcher still has a quarter of this
market.

The move that does work: take one single-cell foundation model, probe or fine-tune it on
*your own* task, and report honestly whether it beat a linear baseline. Being the person
who can *evaluate* the hype is worth more than being the five-hundredth person claiming to
build it.

## There is no "biopharma job market"

There are 206 separate hiring events.

| Roles listed by one employer | Employers |
|---|---:|
| exactly 1 | **160** |
| exactly 2 | 43 |
| 3 or more | 3 |

**160 of 206 employers list exactly one role.** You are not applying to a company — you are
applying to *one specific manager's one specific headcount.* If that requisition closes,
that company is gone from your pipeline. No second team, no other req, no internal transfer
to wait for.

Which makes the instinct — *apply to the ten famous companies* — precisely backwards. It
also means a single posting is often the entire computational function of that company,
which is a thing worth knowing *before* you accept it.

Geography compounds it: **170 of 255 roles (67%) are in Boston/Cambridge or the SF Bay
Area.** You are moving to one of two places. *(Substring match on the location field — an
estimate, not a census.)*

## The clock

Assuming you finish around May 2027:

| When | What |
|---|---|
| ~Feb 2027 | File I-765 for OPT at the 90-day mark. Not later. |
| Jul 2027 | OPT begins — 12 months. A **90-day cumulative unemployment cap** applies. |
| Mar 2028 | H-1B lottery, attempt 1. |
| ~Apr 2028 | File STEM-OPT — requires an **E-Verify employer** + I-983 training plan. |
| Jul 2028 | STEM-OPT begins — 24 months, to mid-2030. |
| Mar 2029 / Mar 2030 | Lottery attempts 2 and 3. |
| Jun 2030 | STEM-OPT expires. 60-day grace, then change status or leave. |

At the 25–30% per-year odds the lottery has been running, three tries give a cumulative
**58–66%** chance of selection. Which means a **34–42% chance you strike out on all three**
and arrive in late 2030 with three years of US industry experience you cannot legally use
here. That is not a tail risk. It should shape which job you take in 2027.

Two clock-killers people find out about too late:

- **No E-Verify, no STEM-OPT.** Your 36 months collapse to 12 and your three lottery tries
  collapse to *one*. Verify E-Verify enrollment **before you sign**. A small startup is
  exactly where this bites.
- **The 90-day unemployment cap.** A long unfunded search after graduation eats your status
  directly.

### The cap-exempt hatch, and the misconception that ruins it

The vault holds 205 US academic and institute roles at **97% confirmed sponsorship.**
University and nonprofit H-1Bs are cap-exempt: no lottery, no March window, petitions any
time of year.

Now the part most people get backwards. **A cap-exempt H-1B does not exempt you later.**
Moving from an academic postdoc to a cap-subject industry job still requires winning the
lottery. The postdoc does not launder you into industry.

What it actually buys is time: it converts *"three tries, then leave"* into *"unlimited
tries, no deadline."* That is a genuinely different game — a strategic branch, not a
consolation prize. Run it in parallel from day one, not as a panic move in April 2027.

## What to do, and when

**Now through December 2026: apply to nothing.**

An open requisition today is a team that needs someone *now*. You are available in twelve
months. Applying isn't neutral — at a one-role employer it burns your only shot and leaves
a rejection the ATS remembers.

Do this instead:

- **Resolve the question marks.** Four employers a week for twenty weeks clears the list.
  By New Year you own a spreadsheet where 174 unknowns have collapsed into *sponsors /
  doesn't / still won't say.* Nobody else applying will have that.
- **Mine the 85 live reqs as intelligence, not opportunity.** They are a free, expiring
  corpus of exactly what these teams want. **104 more are explicitly recurring** — a
  recurring req is a promise that this team hires this role again, and it tells you what
  the 2027 version will say.
- **Instrument all 206 employers** with career-page and ATS alerts. In a fragmented market
  the first few qualified applicants define the shortlist.
- **Ship one flagship public artifact.** An HRD/DDR stratifier, end to end: public cohorts,
  packaged repo, containerized, benchmarked against an honest naive baseline, with a model
  card and a preprint. Nothing else this year has the same leverage.
- **Start accruing O-1 evidence now** — reviewing, invited talks, first-author preprints.
  It is the only path that doesn't route through a lottery.

**January through June 2027: the whole ballgame.** Rebuild the CV in January around the
vocabulary the postings actually use. Then apply hard from March, the day the alerts fire,
across **60–80 applications at 70-plus distinct employers** you have already researched and
mostly already emailed a human at. Not ten. Not two hundred sprayed. Run the cap-exempt
academic applications in parallel on their slower clock.

*(That 60–80 is an allocation forced by the fragmentation, not a measured conversion rate.
This dataset contains no hit-rate data and I won't invent one.)*

**The one outreach move that works:** email the *hiring scientist*, not the recruiter.
Comp-bio is small, the teams are tiny, and the person hiring has probably read a paper
adjacent to yours. A hundred and fifty words: who you are, when you finish, and — the entire
point of the email — **one specific, technical, non-obvious observation about their work.**
Recruiters at 160-of-206 companies are generalists who cannot evaluate you. Hiring
scientists can.

## The sweet challenge, and the role that breaks you

75% of these titles are unlevelled IC roles. That is your rung — so learn to read it.

| A stretch that grows you | A stretch that breaks you |
|---|---|
| Title is **Scientist**, **Scientist I**, **Computational Biologist** — the unlevelled IC rung. | **"Staff"** or **"Senior"** at a *small* company: judged against a bar you haven't met, nobody senior to learn from. |
| Science inside your core; the ML asks slightly more than you have today. | An FM-first shop where the science is protein/chemistry — weakest ML person *and* off your domain. |
| ✓ on sponsorship, and a team big enough that you're the **fifth** comp-bio hire, not the first. | The **sole** computational hire. Check the team page before applying. |
| You will publish and build models. | **A "?" sponsor at a sub-50-person startup.** Laid off during OPT, you have 60 days. |

And the dead end: **pipeline engineering dressed as science.** It pays, and it does not
compound — no papers, no models, no O-1 case. For someone who needs an O-1 or a green card
inside 36 months, *a job where you will not publish is not a job. It is a countdown.*

## Method, and what this doesn't tell you

Every figure was computed in Python directly from `comp-bio-jobs-2027.yaml` — 2,134 items,
filtered to positions, US, biopharma sectors. Five analyst agents worked the same frozen
extract, and two adversarial fact-checkers re-verified every claim against it; **24 defects
were caught and corrected before this was written**, including two places where an
unverified "?" had been narrated as a confirmed sponsor. Where an analyst's number
disagreed with the data, the data won.

What this cannot tell you:

- **These are not 2027 requisitions.** Full-time reqs for 2027 don't exist yet. This is the
  market's *structure* — sectors, sponsorship, topics, fragmentation — which is stable, plus
  today's postings as templates.
- **No hit rates.** The vault has no application-outcome data.
- **No live link check was run in this pass.** Some URLs will have rotted.
- **Sponsorship flags are employer-level judgments,** not per-requisition guarantees.
  Verify every one before it matters to you.
